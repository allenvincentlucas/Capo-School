01

What a capo actually does

The capo — from the Italian capotasto, "head-tie" — clamps across the neck and acts as a temporary, movable nut. It shortens the vibrating length of every string, raising their open pitch in half-step (semitone) steps.

Mechanism

New zero fret

Everything behind the capo is muted. The clamped fret becomes the new starting point for all six strings.

Not just training wheels

A real performance tool

Professional players use capos to shift keys instantly, change acoustic tone, and unlock chord voicings that aren't possible open.

Beginner payoff

Skip the barre chords

Because the capo shifts pitch for you, you can play songs in hard keys using only the easy open shapes you already know.

03

Choosing a capo for your guitar

Capos differ in how they clamp and how curved their pad is — and both need to match your guitar.

Fast

Trigger / spring-loaded

One-handed and quick — great live. Fixed tension can pull light strings sharp.

Precise

Screw-fit

A dial sets exact pressure. Slower to apply, but easiest on tuning and frets.

Cheap

Strap / toggle

Light and inexpensive, but wears out fast and can clamp unevenly.

Premium

Cradle / yoke

Wraps the whole neck for very even pressure; parks behind the nut when idle.

Specialty

Partial capo

Clamps only 3–5 strings to fake an open tuning like DADGAD.

Emergency only

Pencil + rubber band

Illustrates the concept — not a long-term solution.

04

Applying the capo, step by step

This is the one sequence in the lesson where order really matters — follow it in order every time you clamp on.

  1. Pre-tune the guitar

    Tune to pitch with a chromatic tuner before the capo ever touches the neck. This gives you a stable baseline.

  2. Open the capo fully

    Loosen the screw or squeeze the trigger all the way. Hold it parallel to the fret wire.

  3. Position it behind the fret

    Sit the pad 5–10mm behind the target fret wire, on the body side — never on top of the metal.

  4. Clamp straight down

    No sideways drag. Dragging stretches strings and ruins intonation.

  5. Set the tension

    Tighten only until the buzz disappears. Overtightening pulls strings sharp and wears your frets.

  6. Re-tune

    Check the tuner again once the capo is clamped and make any small corrections.

05

Transposition theory

Every fret you move the capo up raises your open shapes by one half-step. Use that in either direction: to raise a song's key, or to keep the original key while swapping to easier shapes.

Sounds like C

Example from the lesson: song in the key of C, capo on fret 5 (+5 semitones) → shapes move down 5 semitones. Absolute C is played as a G shape, Dm7 as Am7, G7 as D7, and G/B as D/F#.

Before you go

  • Buy quality gear. Skip uneven elastic straps or non-adjustable springs — a screw-fit or precision trigger capo will hold its tension properly.

  • Tune before and after. Every time. Capos shift pitch slightly as they clamp.

  • Don't let the capo replace barre chords forever. Keep building that strength alongside your capo playing.

  • Experiment. Try different capo positions with other musicians for layered arrangements, or move it high up the neck for a bright, mandolin-like tone.
